\section{Cross-Fitted Orthogonal Estimation}

Partition the auction sample into folds $I_1,\ldots,I_K$. For each fold $k$,
train the WGAN-GP and estimate all nuisance functions using observations outside
$I_k$. Denote the resulting nuisance estimate by $\widehat\eta^{(-k)}$.

Sample splitting by itself is not sufficient for DML in this application. The
fold-averaged plug-in $\overline r_{\mathrm{FA}}$ introduced in Section~5 uses
the same training partitions but does not evaluate an observation-level score
on $I_k$. It is therefore an ensemble benchmark. Cross-fitting acquires its
inferential role only when each held-out observation enters the score below.

Let $\Phi$ and $\phi$ denote the standard normal CDF and density. For fixed
bandwidth $h$, define the observable signals
\begin{equation}
S_h(B;\theta)=\Phi\!\left(\frac{\theta-B}{h}\right),
\qquad
K_h(B;\theta)=\frac{1}{h}\phi\!\left(\frac{\theta-B}{h}\right),
\end{equation}
and the local nuisance vector
$\eta_h(\theta)=(A_h(\theta),G_h(\theta))$, where
$A_h(\theta)=\mathbb E[S_h(B;\theta)]$ and
$G_h(\theta)=\mathbb E[K_h(B;\theta)]$. The regularized reserve moment is
\begin{equation}
m_h(\theta,A,G)=1-A^{1/N}-\frac{\theta}{N}A^{1/N-1}G.
\label{eq:regularized-reserve-moment}
\end{equation}
The fixed-bandwidth target $\theta_{0,h}$ is the selected zero of
$m_h(\theta,A_h(\theta),G_h(\theta))$. It need not equal the exact Myerson
reserve $r_0$; approximation of $r_0$ requires a separate shrinking-bandwidth
argument.

The derivatives needed for the locally robust correction are
\begin{align}
m_A(\theta,A,G)
&=-\frac{1}{N}A^{1/N-1}
-\frac{\theta}{N}\left(\frac{1}{N}-1\right)A^{1/N-2}G,\\
m_G(\theta,A,G)&=-\frac{\theta}{N}A^{1/N-1}.
\end{align}
The observation-level score is
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\psi_h(B;\theta,A,G)
={}&m_h(\theta,A,G)
+m_A(\theta,A,G)\{S_h(B;\theta)-A\}\\
&+m_G(\theta,A,G)\{K_h(B;\theta)-G\}.
\end{split}
\label{eq:orthogonal-reserve-score}
\end{equation}
Because the two residuals have mean zero at the truth, differentiation of the
score expectation adds $-m_A$ and $-m_G$ to the derivatives of the first term.
Hence
\begin{equation}
\left.\partial_{(A,G)}
\mathbb E\left[\psi_h(B;\theta_{0,h},A,G)\right]
\right|_{(A,G)=\eta_{0,h}(\theta_{0,h})}=0.
\end{equation}

The estimator solves
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^K\sum_{i\in I_k}
\psi_h\!\left(B_i;\widehat\theta_h,
\widehat A_h^{(-k)}(\widehat\theta_h),
\widehat G_h^{(-k)}(\widehat\theta_h)\right)=0.
\end{equation}
Here the fold-specific nuisances are computed from maximum bids simulated by
the WGAN trained outside fold $k$. A numerical derivative of the cross-fitted
mean score estimates the Jacobian, and the empirical score variance supplies
the conventional influence-function standard error. Validity still requires
the second-order nuisance remainder and generator simulation error to be
smaller than sampling error. The pilot below directly tests, rather than
assumes, this requirement.

To diagnose a failure of the WGAN-based nuisance, I also evaluate two versions
of the identical score. The empirical-local version replaces generated maxima
with observed maxima from the corresponding training fold when estimating
$(A_h,G_h)$. The oracle version supplies their population values from the known
Monte Carlo DGP. Agreement between these two versions, together with failure of
the WGAN version, isolates the problem in nuisance approximation rather than in
equation~\eqref{eq:orthogonal-reserve-score}.

Inference for the exact reserve adds a separate bandwidth problem. Under local
smoothness, Gaussian regularization produces a leading approximation bias of
order $h^2$, while the density component of the score has stochastic scale of
order $(nh)^{-1/2}$. A heuristic undersmoothing requirement is therefore
$nh^5\to0$, together with $nh\to\infty$. For a power rule $h_n\propto
n^{-\alpha}$, this suggests $1/5<\alpha<1$. These rate inequalities do not
guarantee stable finite-sample root selection, especially because the maximum-
bid CDF is small near the reserve. The simulation below reports both coverage
of $\theta_{0,h_n}$ and coverage of $r_0$ rather than treating them as the same
target.

To remove the leading smoothing bias without forcing $h_n$ into the unstable
region, suppose the selected population root admits the local expansion

\begin{equation}
\theta_{0,h}=r_0+b_2h^2+O(h^4).
\end{equation}

For a fixed ratio $\lambda>1$, define the Richardson estimator

\begin{equation}
\widehat r_{\mathrm R}
=\frac{\lambda^2\widehat\theta_h-
\widehat\theta_{\lambda h}}{\lambda^2-1}.
\label{eq:richardson-reserve}
\end{equation}

The $O(h^2)$ term cancels in its population analogue. Both estimates use the
same folds and observations, so their dependence must enter inference. If
$\widehat\varphi_{i,h}$ and $\widehat\varphi_{i,\lambda h}$ are their estimated
influence values, the corrected influence value is

\begin{equation}
\widehat\varphi_{i,\mathrm R}
=\frac{\lambda^2\widehat\varphi_{i,h}-
\widehat\varphi_{i,\lambda h}}{\lambda^2-1},
\qquad
\widehat{\mathrm{se}}(\widehat r_{\mathrm R})
=\frac{\widehat{\mathrm{sd}}(\widehat\varphi_{i,\mathrm R})}{\sqrt n}.
\end{equation}

The implementation sets $\lambda=\sqrt{2}$, so
$\widehat r_{\mathrm R}=2\widehat\theta_h-
\widehat\theta_{\sqrt{2}h}$. It selects the smoother root first and uses it as
the reference for the smaller-bandwidth root. This continuation rule reduces
arbitrary root switching but does not eliminate multiple-root uncertainty.
