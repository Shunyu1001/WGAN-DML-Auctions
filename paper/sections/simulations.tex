\section{Monte Carlo Design}

\subsection{Direct-inversion benchmark}

The first experiment fixes the baseline model before introducing WGAN-GP or
DML. Values are lognormal with $\log V_{ij}\sim\mathcal N(0,0.5^2)$, every
auction has $N=5$ bidders, and the econometrician observes only the maximum.
For each sample size $n\in\{200,500,2000,10000\}$, I generate 500 Monte Carlo
samples. The random seed is fixed at 20260716. In every replication,
$\widehat F_{\mathrm{DI}}=\widehat H_n^{1/N}$ and the reserve maximizes
$r\{1-\widehat F_{\mathrm{DI}}(r)\}$ over the observed maxima.

\subsection{Structural WGAN-GP pilot}

I next replace direct inversion with the structural generator described in
Section~5 while retaining the same lognormal DGP and $N=5$. The pilot uses five
training seeds at each $n\in\{500,2000,10000\}$. Each run has 1,200 generator
updates, five critic updates per generator update, a batch size of 512, and a
gradient-penalty weight of 0.1. The generator checkpoint minimizes the
observable maximum-bid Wasserstein distance. These five repetitions diagnose
the pipeline and its failure modes; the final Monte Carlo will use substantially
more repetitions.

Table~\ref{tab:wgan-gp-pilot} shows that the WGAN-GP closely matches the
observable maximum distribution, with mean $W_1$ between 0.034 and 0.039, but
retains a reserve-price bias of roughly $-0.04$ in this pilot. The left panel
of Figure~\ref{fig:wgan-gp-pilot} explains the discrepancy: the learned latent
CDF is close over much of its support but misses part of the lower tail that is
informative about the reserve. At $n=500$, WGAN-GP reduces RMSE relative to
direct inversion from 0.0757 to 0.0687; at the larger pilot sample sizes it
does not improve RMSE. This is evidence that observable distributional fit is
not sufficient for accurate scalar estimation. It motivates the next
cross-fitting and orthogonal-score experiments, without presuming that those
steps will eliminate the bias.

\subsection{Fold-averaged WGAN precursor}

I next partition each Monte Carlo sample into three folds. For each fold, the
structural WGAN-GP is trained on the other two folds using the same architecture
and 1,200-step training convention. The plug-in reserves from the three
training samples are averaged. Held-out auctions are used only to calculate an
out-of-fold maximum-bid Wasserstein diagnostic. Thus, this experiment isolates
sample splitting and generator averaging from the orthogonal-score correction.

Table~\ref{tab:crossfit-wgan-pilot} shows that fold averaging reduces WGAN RMSE
from 0.0687 to 0.0368 at $n=500$, from 0.0579 to 0.0270 at $n=2000$, and from
0.0612 to 0.0274 at $n=10000$. The gain is mainly lower dispersion across data
and training seeds. Bias remains negative, between $-0.033$ and $-0.019$.
Figure~\ref{fig:crossfit-wgan-pilot} also shows that held-out maximum-bid
$W_1$ falls with sample size, while the reserve does not converge monotonically
in this small pilot. These results support using cross-fitting as the nuisance
training architecture, but they do not attribute the improvement to DML or
claim that sample splitting removes first-order nuisance bias.

\subsection{Next orthogonal-score comparison}

The next experiments will vary the auction sample size, number of bidders,
valuation distribution, degree of tail thickness, auction-level heterogeneity,
and structural misspecification. Candidate valuation distributions include
lognormal, gamma, mixture, and nonregular designs. The comparison will include
direct order-statistic inversion, conventional plug-in estimators, WGAN-GP
plug-in estimation, fold-averaged WGAN-GP, and the orthogonal WGAN-DML
estimator. Evaluation metrics include bias, root mean squared error,
confidence-interval coverage, distributional fit, and revenue regret:
\begin{equation}
R(r_0;F_0)-R(\widehat r;F_0).
\end{equation}
The same Monte Carlo seed policy and the direct benchmark will be retained
when these estimators are added.
