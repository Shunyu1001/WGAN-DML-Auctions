\section{Monte Carlo Design}

\subsection{Direct-inversion benchmark}

The first experiment fixes the baseline model before introducing WGAN-GP or
DML. Values are lognormal with $\log V_{ij}\sim\mathcal N(0,0.5^2)$, every
auction has $N=5$ bidders, and the econometrician observes only the maximum.
For each sample size $n\in\{200,500,2000,10000\}$, I generate 500 Monte Carlo
samples. The random seed is fixed at 20260716. In every replication,
$\widehat F_{\mathrm{DI}}=\widehat H_n^{1/N}$ and the reserve maximizes
$r\{1-\widehat F_{\mathrm{DI}}(r)\}$ over the observed maxima.

\subsection{Structural WGAN-GP pilot}

I next replace direct inversion with the structural generator described in
Section~5 while retaining the same lognormal DGP and $N=5$. The pilot uses five
training seeds at each $n\in\{500,2000,10000\}$. Each run has 1,200 generator
updates, five critic updates per generator update, a batch size of 512, and a
gradient-penalty weight of 0.1. The generator checkpoint minimizes the
observable maximum-bid Wasserstein distance. These five repetitions diagnose
the pipeline and its failure modes; the final Monte Carlo will use substantially
more repetitions.

Table~\ref{tab:wgan-gp-pilot} shows that the WGAN-GP closely matches the
observable maximum distribution, with mean $W_1$ between 0.034 and 0.039, but
retains a reserve-price bias of roughly $-0.04$ in this pilot. The left panel
of Figure~\ref{fig:wgan-gp-pilot} explains the discrepancy: the learned latent
CDF is close over much of its support but misses part of the lower tail that is
informative about the reserve. At $n=500$, WGAN-GP reduces RMSE relative to
direct inversion from 0.0757 to 0.0687; at the larger pilot sample sizes it
does not improve RMSE. This is evidence that observable distributional fit is
not sufficient for accurate scalar estimation. It motivates the next
cross-fitting and orthogonal-score experiments, without presuming that those
steps will eliminate the bias.

\subsection{Fold-averaged WGAN precursor}

I next partition each Monte Carlo sample into three folds. For each fold, the
structural WGAN-GP is trained on the other two folds using the same architecture
and 1,200-step training convention. The plug-in reserves from the three
training samples are averaged. Held-out auctions are used only to calculate an
out-of-fold maximum-bid Wasserstein diagnostic. Thus, this experiment isolates
sample splitting and generator averaging from the orthogonal-score correction.

Table~\ref{tab:crossfit-wgan-pilot} shows that fold averaging reduces WGAN RMSE
from 0.0687 to 0.0368 at $n=500$, from 0.0579 to 0.0270 at $n=2000$, and from
0.0612 to 0.0274 at $n=10000$. The gain is mainly lower dispersion across data
and training seeds. Bias remains negative, between $-0.033$ and $-0.019$.
Figure~\ref{fig:crossfit-wgan-pilot} also shows that held-out maximum-bid
$W_1$ falls with sample size, while the reserve does not converge monotonically
in this small pilot. These results support using cross-fitting as the nuisance
training architecture, but they do not attribute the improvement to DML or
claim that sample splitting removes first-order nuisance bias.

\subsection{Observation-level orthogonal-score pilot}

I next implement equation~\eqref{eq:orthogonal-reserve-score} using the same
three folds, WGAN architecture, data seeds, and 1,200-step training convention.
The Gaussian bandwidth is fixed at $h=0.15$, and each training-fold generator
produces 50,000 synthetic maxima for the nuisance moments. Held-out auctions
enter the score through both the smoothed-CDF and kernel-density residuals. The
true fixed-bandwidth target is $\theta_{0,h}=0.8318$, compared with the exact
reserve $r_0=0.7719$; their difference is regularization bias, not estimation
error.

Table~\ref{tab:orthogonal-wgan-dml} rejects the hypothesis that formal
orthogonality alone repairs the current WGAN nuisance fit. The orthogonal
correction raises the fold-averaged estimate by 0.123, 0.122, and 0.145 on
average as $n$ increases. Relative to $r_0$, its RMSE is 0.1355, 0.1046, and
0.1276, compared with 0.0368, 0.0270, and 0.0274 for fold averaging. Coverage
of $r_h$ is 40 percent, 60 percent, and zero. The intervals narrow with $n$
even though the bias does not, producing especially severe undercoverage at
$n=10000$.

This failure is informative. Orthogonality removes the first-order derivative
with respect to $(A_h,G_h)$ at the truth; it does not make a large local
nuisance error small. In this design, $r_0$ lies in the extreme lower tail of
the maximum-bid distribution, and the global WGAN fit does not deliver the
local rate needed to control the second-order remainder. Figure~\ref{fig:orthogonal-wgan-dml}
therefore motivates local tail-weighting, additional observed order statistics,
or a stronger structural restriction before using the influence-function
standard errors for inference.

\subsection{Oracle and empirical-local nuisance ablation}

I next hold the score and bandwidth fixed and change only its nuisance inputs.
The empirical-local estimator calculates $(A_h,G_h)$ directly from maximum bids
in each training fold. The oracle supplies their population values from the
known DGP. Both designs use 200 repetitions per sample size; the WGAN column
retains the five costly training seeds above.

Table~\ref{tab:local-nuisance-ablation} shows that the empirical-local score
nearly reproduces the oracle. Its coverage is 90.0, 94.5, and 95.0 percent as
$n$ increases, compared with 94.0, 96.0, and 95.5 percent under the oracle.
Its RMSE relative to $r_h$ is 0.0350, 0.0171, and 0.0079; the oracle values are
0.0311, 0.0164, and 0.0078. Every replication has a score root on the search
grid. Figure~\ref{fig:local-nuisance-ablation} contrasts this convergence with
the persistent WGAN-nuisance error.

The ablation also quantifies the information problem. The sample contains on
average only 1.05, 5.04, and 25.69 observed maxima at or below the exact reserve
for $n=500,2000,10000$. Nevertheless, direct training-fold moments become
accurate enough under the fixed bandwidth. Thus the earlier failure is not a
failure of the orthogonal algebra. It is evidence that a globally trained WGAN
is an unnecessarily indirect and currently inaccurate estimator of observable
local score moments. The preferred baseline is a hybrid: use WGAN-GP for the
full latent distribution and structural counterfactuals, but use dedicated
training-fold local moments for reserve inference.

\subsection{Shrinking bandwidth and the exact reserve}

The previous experiment validates inference for a fixed regularized target.
I now set
\begin{equation}
h_n=0.15\left(\frac{n}{500}\right)^{-\alpha},
\qquad \alpha\in\{0,0.2,0.35,0.5\},
\end{equation}
and repeat the empirical-local estimator 200 times. The comparison holds the
data seeds and score construction fixed. It reports coverage of both
$r_{h_n}$ and the exact reserve $r_0$, so decreasing regularization bias cannot
be confused with improved score estimation.

Table~\ref{tab:bandwidth-path} shows why fixed-$h$ and exact-target inference
must remain distinct. Fixed-$h$ coverage of $r_0$ is 63.5 percent at $n=500$,
1 percent at $n=2000$, and zero at $n=10000$, even though its coverage of
$r_h$ approaches 95 percent. The $n^{-0.2}$ rule reduces exact-target RMSE to
0.0327 at $n=10000$, the smallest value among the four paths there, but exact
coverage is only 60 percent.

The more aggressive $n^{-0.35}$ rule raises exact-target coverage to 82 percent
at both $n=2000$ and $n=10000$, still below nominal. At $n=10000$, 10.5 percent
of samples have multiple score roots. Shrinking at $n^{-0.5}$ is worse: the
multiple-root rate reaches 69 percent, exact-target RMSE rises to 0.0475, and
coverage falls to 73 percent. Figure~\ref{fig:bandwidth-path} summarizes the
bias-variance and root-selection trade-off. No tested power rule simultaneously
achieves low RMSE, nominal coverage of $r_0$, and stable roots in this sample
range. Fixed-bandwidth inference for $r_h$ is therefore the current defensible
baseline; exact-reserve inference requires explicit bias correction or a
confidence set that accounts for regularization and root uncertainty.

For the stable $n^{-0.2}$ path, bias correction improves all three exact-target
metrics. At $n=2000$, coverage rises from 59.0 to 92.5 percent and RMSE falls
from 0.0512 to 0.0420. At $n=10000$, coverage rises from 60.0 to 88.5 percent
and RMSE falls from 0.0327 to 0.0296. The extrapolated population-target bias
there is 0.0080, compared with 0.0252 for the uncorrected target. No replication
on this path has a missing or multiple root.

The faster $n^{-0.35}$ path does not benefit in the same way. Correction raises
RMSE from 0.0482 to 0.0532 at $n=2000$ and from 0.0354 to 0.0459 at $n=10000$.
At the largest sample size, 30.5 percent of replications have multiple roots at
one of the two bandwidths, and corrected coverage is only 84.0 percent. Thus
Richardson extrapolation is not a substitute for root stability. The
$n^{-0.2}$ correction is the preferred exact-target prototype, but its 88.5
percent coverage at $n=10000$ remains below nominal. This points to residual
$O(h^4)$ bias, nonlinear root selection, or non-Gaussian score behavior as the
next inferential problem.

On the stable $n^{-0.2}$ path, the selected-root bias envelope attains 96.5
percent exact-reserve coverage at all three sample sizes. At $n=10000$, this
raises coverage from 88.5 percent for the Richardson Wald interval while
increasing mean length from 0.0998 to 0.1279. Every replication has exactly one
root, so the selected-root and root-robust sets coincide. This evidence assigns
the stable-path undercoverage primarily to remaining higher-order bias or
uncertainty in its estimate, rather than root selection.

The aggressive $n^{-0.35}$ path remains problematic. At $n=10000$, 30.5
percent of samples have multiple roots. The bias envelope raises coverage from
84.0 to 91.5 percent, still below nominal. Taking the union over all root chains
does not improve coverage further, but doubles mean set length from 0.2051 to
0.4064. Thus the omitted roots are mostly economically remote alternatives,
not the source of the remaining coverage failures. The finite-sample evidence
favors the $n^{-0.2}$ three-bandwidth envelope as the exact-target prototype,
while retaining fixed-bandwidth inference as the formal baseline until the
bias expansion and joint root process receive theoretical guarantees.

\subsection{Competition and valuation distributions}

The final experiment varies the number of bidders and the valuation family
without adding another estimator or confidence-interval variant. It crosses
$N\in\{3,5,10\}$ with the baseline lognormal distribution, a thicker-tailed
lognormal distribution, and a two-component lognormal mixture. For each of the
18 design cells, Table~\ref{tab:external-validity} reports 200 replications of
the empirical-local DML estimator with the same fixed Gaussian bandwidth
$h=0.15$ and three-fold cross-fitting. The formal inferential target is the
cell-specific regularized root $r_h$. Exact-reserve RMSE is also reported for
the DML estimate and direct order-statistic inversion so that smoothing bias
is not hidden inside the inferential comparison.

For $N=3$ or $N=5$, target coverage ranges from 92.0 to 96.5 percent across
the three distributions and two sample sizes. The regularized-target RMSE
falls by roughly one half when the number of auctions rises from 2000 to
10000. These results show that the fixed-bandwidth theory is not specific to
the baseline lognormal distribution. They also make clear that valid
inference for $r_h$ does not remove the economically separate approximation
error $r_h-r_0$: that gap is largest in the mixture and increases with the
number of bidders.

The difficult cells have $N=10$. With 2000 auctions, the target coverage is
77.5, 78.0, and 78.5 percent in the baseline, heavy-tail, and mixture designs,
respectively, and between 9.5 and 13.0 percent of samples have multiple roots.
The expected number of observed auction maxima below the exact reserve is at
most two in these cells and essentially zero in the baseline and mixture.
With 10000 auctions, multiple roots disappear and coverage rises to 87.0,
93.5, and 89.5 percent. Thus greater competition makes a low reserve a more
severe lower-tail extrapolation from maxima. Cross-fitting and orthogonality
do not manufacture local information that the order statistic rarely
contains. This is a transparent support limitation, not a generic failure of
global distribution fit.

The exercise remains within symmetric independent private values and known
$N$. It does not establish robustness to endogenous entry, auction
heterogeneity, dependence across bidders, or structural misspecification.
